Abstract

AI-generated moving images now circulate as model demonstrations, short films, festival works and platform-native audiovisual experiments. They are often discussed through automation, copyright, deception or production efficiency, yet these frames do not fully explain how generated images become legible as moving-image culture. This article proposes algorithmic animaticity as a concept for analysing how generative AI systems produce motion, liveliness and screen-world continuity through probabilistic modelling, data-driven synthesis and human selection. Drawing on film theory, animation studies, social semiotics and digital media theory, the article examines Sora demonstrations, Air Head, The Frost, Total Pixel Space, Intracranial Garden (Lu Nei Hua Yuan) and Dang Gui (Getting Home) as symptomatic cases of generative screen culture. The analysis develops three claims concerning animated intention, data indexicality and synthetic bodies. The article treats digital intelligence as a cultural condition in which generative systems organise perception, agency and audiovisual trust, linking generative audiovisual aesthetics to disclosure, trust, institutional evaluation and visual literacy.
