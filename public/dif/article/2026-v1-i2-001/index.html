Abstract

Project-based Learning (PBL) in primary school English often oscillates between two extremes: reducing PBL to contextualized language drills or designing inquiry tasks disconnected from learners' proficiency levels. This tension stems from a fragmented conceptualization of the relationship between the essence of English as a discipline and the intended outcomes of PBL. Grounded in Herbart's subject-centered theory, Second Language Acquisition (SLA) research, and dialectical materialism, this study clarifies the interdependence and dynamic equilibrium between language and inquiry. A four-pronged practical framework is proposed, encompassing cognitive restructuring, design anchored in disciplinary essence, process-oriented assessment, and differentiated scaffolding. A concrete instructional case illustrates the framework's applicability. The study aims to restore the pedagogical integrity of English PBL, fostering the synergistic development of linguistic competence and holistic literacy.
