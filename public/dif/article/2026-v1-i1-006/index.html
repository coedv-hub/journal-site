Abstract

The deep integration of autonomous driving technology and the automotive industry is reshaping global transportation and challenging traditional vehicle legal systems. Major countries and regions have accelerated legislation, but there are disparities in regulatory philosophies, technical standards and liability allocation, causing cross-border legal conflicts and regulatory gaps. This paper uses a comparative law approach to analyze six typical governance models, including the United Nations, the European Union, Germany, the United Kingdom, the United States and Japan, extracting common experiences and core divergences in safety assessment, access management and liability configuration. It then examines structural problems in China's autonomous driving governance, such as the absence of superior legislation, fragmented local rules, imperfect liability systems and insufficient international coordination. Finally, it proposes a governance reconstruction scheme based on "unified legislation, classified regulation and international coordination". It suggests enacting an Autonomous Vehicles Law, establishing a two-tier governance structure, improving the safety assessment system, and participating in international rule-making at the UN level to guarantee the large-scale application of autonomous driving technology in China.
